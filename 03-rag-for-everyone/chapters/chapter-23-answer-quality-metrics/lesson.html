Answer Quality Metrics

Retrieval metrics tell us whether the right evidence arrived. Answer quality metrics tell us whether the final response used that evidence correctly, helpfully, and safely.

How to read this chapter

  • Do not score beauty first: polished unsupported answers are dangerous.
  • Separate quality dimensions: faithfulness, relevance, citations, refusal, completeness, and safety are different checks.
  • Use rubrics: production review needs repeatable scoring, not personal taste.
  • Gate high-risk releases: a model/prompt change should not ship if answer quality regresses on important slices.

A good RAG answer is not the answer that sounds smartest. It is the answer that is supported, useful, calibrated, cited, and safe.
Core ideaAnswer quality metrics evaluate the generated response against the question, evidence, citations, risk level, and expected behavior.
Sub-topic 1 of 12

Why Answer Quality Metrics Matter

A RAG pipeline can retrieve the correct source and still fail. The answer may overstate the policy, miss a condition, cite a source that does not support the sentence, refuse unnecessarily, or answer the wrong question. Answer quality metrics inspect the final response as a product artifact.

Quality north starThe answer should say only what the evidence supports, in a form that solves the user's task.

For production, this is more important than sounding impressive.

01
Extract claims

Identify factual statements in the answer.

02
Check evidence

Confirm each claim is supported by provided context.

03
Check usefulness

Verify the answer addresses the user's real question.

04
Check release risk

Block high-risk regressions and send ambiguous cases for review.

Sub-topic 2 of 12

Real-Life Analogy: Judge and Evidence

Imagine a judge hearing a case. A confident speech is not enough. The judge asks: what evidence supports this claim? Is the evidence relevant? Is the cited document the correct version? Did the argument answer the actual question?

Answer evaluation works the same way. The answer is the argument. Retrieved context is the evidence. Metrics are the courtroom checklist.

Sub-topic 3 of 12

Faithfulness

Faithfulness asks whether the answer stays inside the evidence. If the evidence says "VP approval is required after 30 days" and the answer says "manager approval is enough," faithfulness failed.

Good

Preserves facts, dates, exceptions, and conditions from evidence.

Bad

Adds unsupported thresholds, names, dates, or guarantees.

Production check

Use claim extraction plus support review for high-risk answers.

Sub-topic 4 of 12

Groundedness

Groundedness checks whether the answer can be traced back to retrieved context. Faithfulness asks, "Is it supported?" Groundedness asks, "Can we point to where it is supported?"

Groundedness check

answer claim
  -> supporting citation id
  -> source chunk
  -> source document
  -> version and authority metadata
Sub-topic 5 of 12

Answer Relevance

An answer can be faithful but still not helpful. If the user asks for refund exceptions and the answer gives a general refund-policy summary, relevance is weak. Score whether the answer resolves the user's actual task.

Sub-topic 6 of 12

Citation Correctness

Citation correctness is deeper than citation presence. The cited source must directly support the sentence. This is one of the biggest gaps in shallow RAG demos.

Citation test levels
  • Level 1: citation exists.
  • Level 2: citation is close to the claim.
  • Level 3: cited source supports the exact claim.
  • Level 4: cited source is current, authoritative, and accessible.
Sub-topic 7 of 12

Refusal Correctness

RAG systems can fail by answering when they should refuse, or refusing when they should answer. Track both missed refusals and false refusals. Both hurt trust in different ways.

Sub-topic 8 of 12

Rubric Design

A useful rubric has clear fields: faithfulness, relevance, citation correctness, completeness, refusal correctness, tone, and safety. Keep scoring simple: pass, warn, block, or 0-2 per dimension.

Sub-topic 9 of 12

Human Review

Use human review for high-risk and ambiguous cases: legal, finance, medical, security, executive policy, and customer-impacting answers. Human review should calibrate automated metrics, not replace all automation.

Sub-topic 10 of 12

Release Gates

A prompt, model, retriever, or index release should be blocked if it introduces high-risk unsupported claims, citation-support regressions, unsafe refusals, or known incident replays.

Sub-topic 11 of 12

Common Mistakes

M01

Scoring only fluency

A fluent answer can still be unsupported.

M02

Trusting citations blindly

Citations must support the exact claim.

M03

No false-refusal metric

Overly cautious assistants can become unusable.

M04

No rubric calibration

Reviewers score inconsistently without examples.

Sub-topic 12 of 12

Project Roadmap

Build an answer quality scorer that receives question, evidence, answer, citations, and risk. It should return faithfulness, relevance, citation correctness, refusal correctness, score, and release gate.
