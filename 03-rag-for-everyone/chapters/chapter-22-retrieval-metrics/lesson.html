Retrieval Metrics

Retrieval metrics tell you whether the right evidence reached the prompt. If retrieval fails, the generator is forced to guess, refuse, or answer from weak context.

How to read this chapter

  • Measure before generation: answer quality starts with evidence quality.
  • Use gold evidence: metrics need known relevant chunks, documents, or citations.
  • Slice failures: track metrics by domain, query type, risk, tenant, and source format.
  • Gate releases: retrieval changes should not ship if key metrics regress.

You cannot fix retrieval by staring only at final answers. You need to know whether the right evidence was retrieved, where it ranked, and what pushed it out.
Core ideaRetrieval metrics compare retrieved results against known relevant evidence so teams can tune search, reranking, filters, chunking, and indexing with confidence.
Sub-topic 1 of 15

Why Retrieval Metrics Matter

At a glance
  • Retrieval determines what facts the model can use.
  • Metrics expose missing, low-ranked, duplicate, stale, or filtered-out evidence.
  • Different metrics answer different questions.
  • Production teams use metrics as release gates and debugging signals.

A RAG answer can be bad for many reasons: the prompt may be weak, the model may ignore evidence, or the retrieved context may be wrong. Retrieval metrics isolate the retrieval step. They answer: did we fetch the source that could answer the question?

Retrieval metric north starMeasure whether the right evidence appears early enough to be used.

The best metric set tells you both recall and rank quality, then shows which slice is failing.

01
Label gold evidence

Define which chunk, document, or passage should support each query.

02
Run retrieval

Capture result ids, rank, score, filters, route, and index version.

03
Compute metrics

Use Recall@K, Precision@K, MRR, hit rate, and nDCG where useful.

04
Act on slices

Tune chunking, filters, hybrid routing, reranking, or index release by failure type.

Sub-topic 2 of 15

Real-Life Analogy: Search Party

Imagine a search party looking for one important file in a giant archive. Recall asks, "Did the team find the file at all?" MRR asks, "How quickly did they find it?" Precision asks, "How much junk did they bring back with it?"

A senior engineer does not ask only one question. If the file appears at rank 40, recall may look decent, but the prompt may never see it. Rank matters.

Sub-topic 3 of 15

Gold Evidence

Gold evidence is the expected source support for a query. It may be a document id, chunk id, paragraph span, table row, or citation id. Without gold evidence, retrieval metrics become vague.

Chunk-level gold

Precise and useful for tuning retrieval, but requires careful labeling.

Document-level gold

Easier to label, but can hide wrong-section retrieval.

Multi-gold

Some questions require several sources, such as policy plus exception addendum.

Sub-topic 4 of 15

Recall@K

Recall@K asks whether the relevant evidence appeared in the top K retrieved results. It is usually the first retrieval metric to track because missing evidence cannot be fixed later by prompting.

Recall@5 intuition

gold chunks: S2, S9
retrieved top 5: S1, S2, S4, S8, S9
Recall@5 = 2 / 2 = 1.0
Sub-topic 5 of 15

Precision@K

Precision@K asks how many of the top K results are relevant. High recall with low precision can still hurt the model because noisy chunks crowd the prompt.

High recall, low precision

The right chunk is present, but surrounded by noise.

Low recall, high precision

The system returns clean results, but misses important evidence.

Trade-off

Candidate pools may optimize recall, final context should optimize precision.

Sub-topic 6 of 15

Mean Reciprocal Rank

MRR rewards systems that put the first relevant result near the top. If the first relevant chunk appears at rank 1, reciprocal rank is 1.0. At rank 5, it is 0.2.

MRR intuition

query A first relevant rank = 1 -> 1/1 = 1.0
query B first relevant rank = 4 -> 1/4 = 0.25
MRR = average reciprocal rank across queries
Sub-topic 7 of 15

nDCG

nDCG is useful when relevance has grades, such as perfect, partial, weak, and irrelevant. It rewards highly relevant evidence appearing early.

When nDCG helps
  • Multiple chunks can partially answer a question.
  • Some sources are authoritative and others are only supporting notes.
  • Search quality needs graded relevance, not yes/no labels.
  • Reranking changes order more than candidate presence.
Sub-topic 8 of 15

Hit Rate

Hit rate asks whether at least one relevant result was retrieved. It is simple and helpful for dashboards, but it can hide rank and completeness problems.

Good for

Fast health checks and broad trend monitoring.

Weakness

A hit at rank 50 may not help final answer quality.

Use with

MRR, Recall@K, and final context precision.

Sub-topic 9 of 15

Failure Slices

Average metrics can lie. A system may look fine overall while failing legal queries, long documents, PDF tables, exact IDs, or a specific tenant.

Domain

Policy vs support

Different corpora often need different retrieval strategies.

Query type

Exact vs semantic

IDs and clauses may need sparse or hybrid search.

Source

PDF vs markdown

Parsing quality can drive retrieval quality.

Risk

Low vs high

High-risk flows need stricter gates and deeper analysis.

A production dashboard should show trends, not just one score. Track metrics by index version, retriever route, embedding model, reranker version, and source freshness.

Quality

Recall@K, MRR, nDCG, citation support after generation.

Latency, timeout rate, empty retrieval rate, filter drop rate.

Sub-topic 12 of 15

Release Gates

Retrieval changes should pass gates before production rollout. A new embedding model, chunking strategy, filter, or reranker can improve one slice and break another.

Gate example
  • Recall@10 must not drop on high-risk policy queries.
  • MRR must improve or remain stable for common support questions.
  • Exact-id query hit rate must stay above threshold.
  • Stale-source hit rate must not increase.
  • Latency must stay within product budget.
Sub-topic 13 of 15

Common Mistakes

M01

Only measuring final answers

Symptom: team cannot tell whether retrieval or generation failed.
Better move: measure retrieval before context injection and generation.
M02

No gold labels

Symptom: retrieval quality becomes subjective.
Better move: create chunk-level or document-level gold evidence.
M03

Tracking only Recall@K

Symptom: gold evidence exists but is buried under noisy results.
Better move: add MRR, precision, and rank analysis.
M04

Ignoring slices

Symptom: average metric looks fine while one domain fails badly.
Better move: slice by domain, query type, source format, tenant, and risk.
M05

Comparing different test sets

Symptom: metric movement is meaningless.
Better move: compare versions on the same benchmark set.
M06

Forgetting filter failures

Symptom: search seems bad, but metadata filters removed gold evidence.
Better move: log filters, candidate pools, and filtered-out gold ids.
M07

No freshness metric

Symptom: retrieval finds old but relevant-looking content.
Better move: track stale hit rate and source version correctness.
M08

Metric without action

Symptom: dashboard exists but no one knows what to fix.
Better move: connect each metric to tuning actions and release gates.
Sub-topic 14 of 15

Interview-Level Understanding

A strong answer sounds like this: "I evaluate retrieval separately from generation. I label gold evidence, run benchmark queries, compute Recall@K, Precision@K, MRR, hit rate, and sometimes nDCG, then slice by domain and query type. I use metrics to decide whether to tune chunking, filters, hybrid search, reranking, or index releases."

Sub-topic 15 of 15

Points To Remember

  • Retrieval metrics answer whether the right evidence reached the system.
  • Recall@K measures candidate presence; MRR measures rank quality.
  • Precision matters because noisy context hurts generation.
  • Average scores must be sliced by domain, query type, source, and risk.
  • Metrics should drive release gates and concrete tuning actions.
