Interview Questions: Retrieval Metrics

Use these questions to practice explaining retrieval evaluation like someone who has operated a RAG system, not only read about one.

Q01

Why evaluate retrieval separately from generation?

Because a bad answer may be caused by missing evidence, buried evidence, noisy evidence, stale evidence, or generation behavior. Retrieval metrics isolate the evidence layer before the model writes anything.

Q02

What is Recall@K?

It is the fraction of expected gold evidence retrieved within the top K results. It answers: did the retriever find the evidence early enough to be available?

Q03

What is Precision@K?

It is the fraction of top K retrieved results that are relevant. It helps detect noisy final context that can distract the model or waste tokens.

Q04

What does MRR tell you that recall does not?

MRR tells you how early the first relevant result appears. Recall can look good even when the correct evidence is ranked too low for the final context.

Q05

When would you use nDCG?

Use it when relevance is graded. A direct policy clause should score higher than a background FAQ, and nDCG rewards ranking stronger evidence earlier.

Q06

What is gold evidence?

Gold evidence is the labeled source, chunk, span, or table row expected to support an answer. It turns retrieval evaluation from opinion into measurement.

Q07

How do you debug low recall?

Inspect corpus coverage, parsing, chunking, embeddings, sparse search, query rewriting, metadata filters, and index freshness. Low recall means candidate discovery is weak.

Q08

How do you debug high recall but low MRR?

The system finds the evidence but ranks it too low. Inspect fusion, reranking, dedupe, final-k cutoff, and whether query rewriting is changing intent.

Q09

Why slice retrieval metrics?

Averages hide pain. Exact-term support, PDF tables, legal policies, and finance workflows can fail differently even when overall Recall@10 looks healthy.

Q10

What is filtered-out gold?

It is relevant evidence removed by metadata, access, tenant, freshness, or policy filters. It should be reported separately because the fix may be filter logic, not search quality.

Q11

What should a retrieval dashboard include?

Recall@K, Precision@K, MRR, nDCG where needed, hit rate, latency, empty retrieval rate, filter drop rate, stale hits, source freshness, index version, and route version.

Q12

How should metrics become release gates?

Define thresholds by risk and slice. A high-risk legal or finance regression should block even if the average metric improves.

Q13

What is the mistake in only measuring Recall@50?

It can hide that the evidence is ranked too low for the final prompt. If your answer uses top 5, Recall@50 may give false comfort.

Q14

How do retrieval metrics connect to answer evaluation?

Retrieval metrics explain whether the answer had enough source support. Answer evaluation then checks faithfulness, completeness, tone, refusal behavior, and citation quality.

Q15

Give a senior-level summary.

I would label gold evidence, evaluate retrieval before generation, compute multiple metrics, slice them by workflow and risk, inspect failures through traces, and use the results as release gates tied to concrete engineering actions.
