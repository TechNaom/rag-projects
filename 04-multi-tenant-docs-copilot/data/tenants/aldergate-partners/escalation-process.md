# Aldergate Partners — Escalation Process

**Doc ID:** AP-ESC-004
**Audience:** All consultants and engagement managers
**Owner:** Delivery Operations
**Last updated:** February 2026

## 1. When to Escalate
Escalate whenever an issue threatens scope, timeline, budget, quality, or the
client relationship and cannot be resolved at the working level within one
business day. Escalating early is a sign of good judgment, not failure.
Consultants are explicitly encouraged to raise concerns before they become
problems.

## 2. The Escalation Path
Issues follow a defined path: consultant to engagement manager, engagement
manager to responsible Principal, Principal to the account Partner. Each level
has one business day to resolve or advance the issue. Skipping a level is only
permitted for a genuine emergency, such as a data breach or a safety concern,
which goes straight to the Partner and the firm's risk lead.

## 3. Delivery Risks
Delivery risks — anything that might cause a missed milestone — are logged in
the weekly status report with an owner and a mitigation. A risk that has more
than a 50% chance of materializing, or that would delay a milestone by more than
a week, is escalated to the Principal immediately rather than waiting for the
weekly report.

## 4. Client Relationship Issues
If the client sponsor expresses dissatisfaction, the engagement manager notifies
the responsible Partner the same day. The Partner decides whether a relationship
reset conversation is needed. Aldergate treats a single unhappy sponsor as an
account-level issue, not just an engagement issue, because references and
renewals depend on it.

## 5. Commercial Disputes
Disputes over billing, scope, or a change order are handled by the engagement
manager together with the Commercial Team, never by a consultant alone. Payment
more than 60 days late is automatically escalated to the account Partner, who
decides whether to pause delivery until the account is brought current.

## 6. Post-Escalation Review
After any significant escalation is resolved, the engagement manager records a
short note capturing what happened, how it was resolved, and what would prevent
a recurrence. These notes feed the quarterly delivery review so the firm learns
from patterns across engagements rather than repeating avoidable mistakes.
